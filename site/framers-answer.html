An Originalist Reframing  ·  Supplement to The Proposal

The Framers' Answer

The Constitution already answered the revenue question.

After Dean Steeves  ·  Cited from primary sources

Reframe

The question is not “what replaces the revenue?” — it is “what size of federal government does the Constitution actually authorize?” Asked correctly, the question answers itself. Article I, § 8, cl. 1 already grants ample taxing power, bounded by Roger Sherman's Purpose Clause. No modern statutory proposal is required. The Framers built the answer into the document.

01The Constitutional Grant

Article I, § 8, cl. 1 — Verbatim

“The Congress shall have Power To lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises, to pay the Debts and provide for the common Defence and general Welfare of the United States; but all Duties, Imposts and Excises shall be uniform throughout the United States…”

U.S. Const., art. I, § 8, cl. 1  ·  ratified 1788

Two things are happening in this clause at once. The first half enumerates the federal taxing powers — four categories, exhaustively. The second half does something Hamilton's revenue defense in Federalist No. 36 never makes explicit on its own: it states the purpose for which those powers may be exercised. Without the second half, the first half has no end. With it, every federal tax must serve one of three constitutionally enumerated objects.

02The Four Enumerated Powers

Each category attaches to something discrete — a person or property, a transaction, an article of trade, or an activity or privilege. The list is closed. There is no fifth.

Direct

I

Taxes

Direct taxes laid on persons or property. Must be apportioned among the States by population (Art. I, § 9).

Indirect

II

Duties

Imposed on specific transactions — most often the importation of goods. Must be uniform throughout the United States.

Indirect

III

Imposts

Charges on imported articles as they cross the national border. Must be uniform throughout the United States.

Indirect

IV

Excises

Taxes on the manufacture, sale, or use of enumerated articles, or on state-granted privileges. Must be uniform throughout the United States.

Hamilton, Federalist No. 36: indirect taxes are “duties and excises on articles of consumption.” The framework is closed because the Framers intended it to be closed.

03Roger Sherman and the Purpose Clause

The taxing power did not arrive in Article I, § 8, cl. 1 in its final form. The historical sequence at the Convention establishes both the grant and the limit:

  • ■Committee of Detailproposed the four enumerated taxing powers (early August 1787)
  • ■Roger Shermanproposed the Purpose Clause to limit those powers to specific ends
  • ■Committee of Elevenincorporated the Purpose Clause into Article I, § 8, cl. 1 (early September 1787)

The Purpose Clause is not ornamental and not aspirational. It is operative. The Framers granted the taxing power and, in the same clause, told the federal government what that power could and could not be used for: to pay the debts, to provide for the common Defence, and to promote the general Welfare of the United States — understood as the People's Union, not the government's own expansion and not the enrichment of any private interest.

The three permitted ends

i.   To pay the Debts.

ii.   To provide for the common Defence.

iii.   To promote the general Welfare of the United States.

Three ends. No fourth. The clause is a grant and a fence.

04The Reframed Question

The Wrong Question

“What replaces the revenue if the Sixteenth Amendment is repealed?”

The wrong question presumes a federal footprint the Constitution never authorized. It asks how the present scale of government is to be financed, not whether the present scale is constitutional. It treats the modern federal budget as a fixed fact and the Constitution as a problem to be worked around.

The Right Question

“What size of federal government does the Constitution actually authorize?”

The right question lets the document do its work. The enumerated powers of Article I, § 8 are ample when the federal government is constrained to its enumerated purposes. Repealing the Sixteenth Amendment does not create a revenue gap; it re-imposes the original constitutional discipline that produced the question of scale in the first place.

05The Bankers' Intrusion

The case for “more” — for revenue beyond what Article I, § 8 authorizes — did not arise inside the constitutional design. It arose from outside it. The 1913 statutory window is the inflection point: the Sixteenth Amendment ratified 3 February 1913, the Revenue Act of 1913 signed 3 October 1913, and the Federal Reserve Act signed 23 December 1913 — with the First World War breaking out the following summer. The income tax became the service mechanism for a debt architecture the Framers' Article I, § 10 specie requirement was designed to prevent.

A fuller treatment of the monetary dimension — Article I, § 9 (apportionment) and Article I, § 10, cl. 1 (gold and silver as the only tender) as twin structural restraints, both removed within a single generation — will be developed in a forthcoming Part II.

06What This Asks of the Reader

Step

I

Expose the abuse.

Document precisely how the federal government manufactured the Sixteenth Amendment and the income tax it established. Cite primary sources. Refuse the modern reframings.

Step

II

Direct lawful pressure.

Article V is the constitutional remedy. The Twenty-First Amendment (1933) repealed the Eighteenth by state convention. The same mechanism is available now.

Step

III

Restore the original grant.

Repeal the Sixteenth. Return federal taxation to Article I, § 8, cl. 1, bounded by the Purpose Clause. The grant is sufficient when adhered to.

07A Position Statement

“I am not Mr. ‘Fix It’ for the government. I am someone who exposes its unconstitutional and unlawful behavior. The first step to changing the government's unconstitutional behavior is to expose it. The People's Energy creates the change.” — Dean Steeves

This page does not propose a replacement. The Constitution does not require one. The work is exposure, documentation, and lawful pressure. The Framers' grant in Article I, § 8, cl. 1 — bounded by Sherman's Purpose Clause — is the complete answer to a question the present federal government would prefer not to face.

Three Framings  ·  One Project

Read this alongside The Proposal and On H.R. 25.

The Proposal presents a modern statutory pathway (H.R. 25, the FairTax Act) as one available legislative vehicle. This page presents the originalist reframing: the Constitution itself, properly read and properly observed, requires no statutory replacement at all. On H.R. 25 presents the critical review: the FairTax Act, read in primary source, is itself unconstitutional. All three are offered. Readers may judge among them.
