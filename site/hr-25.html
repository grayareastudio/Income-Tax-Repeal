A Critical Review  ·  Third Supplement

On H.R. 25

A constitutional review of the FairTax Act.

After Dean Steeves  ·  132 pages, read in primary source

In One Sentence

Having read H.R. 25 in primary source, the conclusion is direct: the FairTax Act is not a constitutional substitute for the federal income tax. It is a fresh manufacture of the same unconstitutional pattern — a sweeping ad valorem excise on consumption that defies the Framers' public meaning of “excise” — on a more aggressive footing than the income tax it would replace.

01The Bill at a Glance

H.R. 25 — The FairTax Act

Reintroduced every Congress since 1999. 132 pages. Imposes a national tax on consumption at the point of sale, replacing several federal direct- and income-based revenue mechanisms with a single broad-based transactional levy.

Cited from primary source. Section references in the analysis below are to the bill as introduced in the 119th Congress.

02What the Bill Taxes

The taxable base is broad. Reading the bill in full, the categories below are taxed at the federal level under the proposed regime:

Category

Rent and Housing.

Consumer rent payments for apartments, houses, and other real property are explicitly taxable. New home sales and improvements are taxed. Sales of existing/used homes are not. Imputed rent on owner-occupied housing is exempt.

Category

New Tangible Goods.

Clothing. Food and groceries. Electronics. New vehicles. Furniture. Appliances. The full domestic retail base on first sale.

Category

Personal Services.

Health care and medical services. Legal services. Financial services. Auto repairs. Haircuts. The fee-based service economy is treated as a taxable transaction.

Category

Financial Intermediation.

Many fees and a portion of interest — including, in some cases, credit-card and mortgage interest above a Treasury-determined basic rate — are reached as “implicit” financial-intermediation services.

Category

Other Consumption.

Utilities (where not for business use). Entertainment and dining out. Personal travel and personal services. Gaming and games of chance, with special-rule treatment.

Category

Imports & Government Use.

Taxable property and services imported into the United States for personal use or consumption are reached at entry. Government and nonprofit consumption is taxed in many cases of final personal use.

The categories above are illustrative and not exhaustive. The bill's drafting reaches the great majority of household economic life, including categories the ordinary reader does not expect to see classified as “excisable” — rent on a primary residence among them.

03The Rate Is a Floor, Not a Ceiling

Stated Rate

23%

tax-inclusive, as written in the bill.

The 23% figure is the rate inclusive of itself — meaning it is quoted as a share of the total amount paid (price plus tax) rather than as an add-on to the pre-tax price.

Effective Rate at the Register

≈ 30%

tax-exclusive, as a consumer experiences it.

A $100 pre-tax purchase becomes a $130 receipt. The bill's 23% headline figure and the 30% lived experience are the same number described two different ways.

The Adjustment Mechanism

The bill ties future rate calculations to federal spending requirements. As the federal government's revenue needs grow, the FairTax rate is structured to grow with them. The 23% / 30% figures are the starting points, not durable ceilings. Expect upward adjustment as a feature, not a bug.

04What It Does Not Eliminate

The bill is sometimes described as if it would replace “the tax system.” It does not. It replaces a defined slice of federal revenue mechanisms. Everything else remains.

Eliminated by H.R. 25

  • Federal individual income tax
  • Federal corporate income tax
  • Federal payroll taxes (FICA / SECA)
  • Federal estate and gift taxes
  • The Internal Revenue Service, as currently constituted

Not Eliminated

  • State income taxes (in 41 states)
  • State and local sales taxes
  • State and local property taxes
  • State estate, gift, and inheritance taxes
  • Federal excises on motor fuels, alcohol, tobacco, firearms, communications
  • Federal customs duties on imports for non-personal use
  • Regulatory fees, licenses, registrations, permits at every level
  • Tolls, court fees, vehicle fees, professional licensing fees

Americans face dozens to over one hundred separate taxes and fees at the federal, state, and local levels, depending on how one counts. The FairTax Act removes a portion of the federal layer. The rest is untouched, and continues to operate alongside the new federal national sales tax.

05The Constitutional Problem

H.R. 25 is structured as a national sales and use tax — an ad valorem excise — and the bill defends its constitutional footing by appeal to the “Excises” category in Article I, § 8, cl. 1. This is precisely the move that requires examination, because it is precisely the move the federal government has used before.

The 1794 Precedent

In 1794, Alexander Hamilton — then Treasury Secretary — engineered the Tax on Private Carriages (1 Stat. 373), an ad valorem extension of the “excise” category beyond articles of consumption to a personally-owned object of household use. Madison characterized such measures as “pernicious innovations.” The government produced the test case Hylton v. United States, 3 U.S. 171 (1796), argued for the government by Hamilton himself, and obtained the constitutional cover it needed. The pattern — use an “excise” label to extend federal taxation past the Framers' public meaning of the term — was established here.

The Framers' public meaning of “excise” is on record. Hamilton, in Federalist No. 36: indirect taxes are “duties and excises on articles of consumption.” Specific articles. Discrete transactions. Enumerable, indirect, self-limiting. An excise was understood as a tax on manufacture, sale, or use of a particular article — not a universal levy on every household economic act.

H.R. 25's national sales tax does not behave as the Framers' excises behaved. It reaches rent on the home a citizen lives in. It reaches medical care, legal counsel, financial services, repairs, haircuts, and the implicit interest in ordinary loans. It reaches imports for personal consumption and, in many cases, government and nonprofit final-use consumption. A tax on essentially every transaction in life, collected at the point of sale, is not an “excise” in the constitutional sense; it is a universal levy with the label “excise” affixed to it. That is the same constitutional sleight Hamilton ran in 1794 — on a vastly broader base.

The argument advanced on this site against the federal income tax applies, with equal force, to H.R. 25.

06Conclusion — Not a Fix, a Fresh Manufacture

H.R. 25 substitutes one unconstitutional regime for another. It eliminates a portion of federal direct extraction (income and payroll), then re-imposes the same extraction in the form of a near-universal transactional levy at a starting effective rate of about 30% — a rate the bill's own machinery is designed to ratchet upward as federal spending grows.

It leaves the dozens to hundred-plus state and local taxes, fees, licenses, tolls, and excises in place. It does not constrain the federal footprint. It does not restore the Purpose Clause. It does not return federal taxation to the four enumerated categories as the Framers wrote and ratified them.

The remedy is unchanged from the rest of this site. Expose the abuse. Direct lawful pressure at the political branches and the states. Repeal the Sixteenth Amendment. And restore federal taxation to Article I, § 8, cl. 1 as bounded by Roger Sherman's Purpose Clause — not to a 132-page replacement that runs the same constitutional pattern at a different door.

07A Position Statement

“What you should be learning from my article is that the government is a criminal organization attempting to bleed us dry; consequently, controlling us.” — Dean Steeves

The FairTax Act is one mechanism. The federal income tax is another. The Federal Reserve, the end of specie redemption, and the manufactured Sixteenth Amendment are others. The pattern is what should hold the reader's attention — not the latest statutory dress in which the pattern arrives.
